About Me

I lived my whole life in Taiwan and took a rare opportunity transferring to a U.S. college at 20.

I’ve always been deeply passionate about “simulation.” That passion came before everything else—physics, economics, or AI/ML—when I solve my teenage existential crisis through dystopian movies and games. [Read the story]

At 13, just after deciding to leave a strict, Asian-type, dogmatic but locally prestigious private school, I suddenly lost all my friends and spent three months locked in my room. Isolated from both family and the outside world, I fell into an existential crisis, unable to find purpose in anything. What eventually saved me were movies and games that delivered philosophy through extreme settings—Detroit: Become Human, Watch Dogs 2, Black Mirror, Fight Club, Interstellar, Joker. They taught me that my greatest fear isn’t suffering—it’s boredom. A perfectly ordered, predictable life felt more dystopian than chaos itself.

Driven by the instinct to escape mediocrity and live a story worth telling, I’ve realized my superpower is creating reality out of imagination—they mostly failed, but I only need one big win to cover all the other loss. This earned me early admission to National Taiwan University, the top university in my country. [How I did it]

At 17, after being eliminated from the national physics olympiad team, I was at risk of losing my college admission, having sacrificed all my coursework for physics. By chance, I discovered that my country hadn’t registered for the European Physics Olympiad, so I decided to register it myself. I gathered my high school classmates, hired an authorized leader for translation, and wrote proposals to fundraise our trip. We kept everything secret—took leave from school, skipped a midterm and a mandatory project presentation—and flew to Slovenia as a self-organized national team. We won two silvers and two bronzes, and no one there could tell we had built the team from scratch.

The year I entered college, ChatGPT came out. That moment marked my transition from physics to AI/ML research. Through this shift, I attended top conferences and met some of the most brilliant minds on Earth—realizing that what truly mattered was finding a way to the U.S., where the frontier was being built. I eventually dropped out of NTU, applied for transfer, and finally made it to the University of Washington in Seattle.

Now in the U.S., closer to the epicenter of today’s technological revolutions—AI, frontier research, and the people building the future—I can see how those sci-fi visions might soon materialize through VR and robotics.

I’m a blend of scientist and strategist—driven to break down problems for insight, while always searching for opportunities and leverage to maximize impact.
